# Teaching Kids to Question AI — A Semester Experiment

## My Claims
Elementary students can develop meaningful AI skepticism through structured "AI debate" activities where they argue against AI-generated answers. The key is not teaching them that AI is wrong, but teaching them that AI's confidence doesn't equal correctness.

## Practice Experience
- The "AI Says / I Say" format worked well: students read an AI response, then wrote their counter-argument
- Students aged 10-11 were surprisingly good at spotting when AI hedged with phrases like "generally speaking" — they called it "the robot being careful"
- What didn't work: asking students to "fact-check" AI directly. They don't have the domain knowledge. What worked was asking "does this answer actually address the question?"
- Unexpected finding: students started applying the same skepticism to textbooks and teacher explanations

## Unresolved Questions
- Is this transferable skepticism genuine critical thinking, or are they just performing what I modeled?
- The students who struggled most were the high achievers — they trusted authoritative-sounding text more. How do you teach skepticism without undermining trust in legitimate sources?
- This worked in a small class (22 students). Would it scale?

## Comparison with Research
- Aligns with Anthropic's AI Fluency framework on the "critical evaluation" axis, but my students are much younger than their target audience
- Contradicts the assumption that AI literacy requires technical understanding first — my students had zero knowledge of how LLMs work, yet developed functional skepticism
- Similar to what Ethan Mollick describes as "AI co-intelligence" but applied to children, not knowledge workers
