```markdown
# TL;DR
Evidence is mixed. Students demonstrate functional skepticism (they can spot weak AI answers), but whether this represents transferable critical thinking or learned performance is unclear. The distinction may matter less than we think — even "performed" skepticism builds the habit.

# Conclusions

1. **Functional AI literacy at age 10-11 is achievable** — students can evaluate AI output quality without understanding how LLMs work. This contradicts frameworks that assume technical knowledge as a prerequisite.

2. **The "genuine vs performed" distinction may be a false binary** — developmental psychology suggests that children learn social and cognitive skills through imitation before internalization. Performed skepticism may be a necessary stage, not a failure mode.

3. **Transfer is the real test** — if students apply the same questioning to non-AI sources (textbooks, news, teacher statements), the skepticism is likely genuine. Early classroom observations suggest this transfer does happen, but over an unclear timeframe.

# Evidence

- In the "AI Says / I Say" classroom experiment, 15 of 22 students could identify when AI responses didn't actually address the question asked — a form of relevance evaluation that doesn't require domain expertise
- 3 students spontaneously applied the same questioning framework to a textbook passage without being prompted
- Anthropic's framework places "critical evaluation" at the Proficiency level (level 3 of 4), suggesting it's an intermediate skill — but their framework assumes adult learners

# Uncertainties

- **Sample size**: One classroom of 22 students over one semester. Replication needed
- **Teacher effect**: The teacher (me) explicitly modeled skepticism. Would students develop it with a less opinionated teacher?
- **Developmental ceiling**: Are 10-11 year olds cognitively ready for genuine epistemic skepticism, or are they operating on pattern matching ("teacher likes it when I question things")?
- **Long-term retention**: No data on whether these skills persist beyond the semester
- **Cultural factor**: This was observed in a specific educational culture. Different classroom norms around authority and questioning may produce different results
```
